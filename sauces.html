Chapter 9 — Advanced Track

🥫 Sauces & Sides

America's barbecue map is really a sauce map — every region's history is written in vinegar, mustard, tomato, or mayonnaise. Learn the seven families, then earn the right to serve none of them.

9.2Three sauces to actually make

Eastern NC vinegar

250 ml cider vinegar · 1 tbsp brown sugar · 1 tsp chili flakes · 1 tsp fine salt · ½ tsp black pepper. Shake in a jar; rest a day. That's it — that's the oldest sauce in America. Splash over pulled pork, never "on the side."

Kansas City lacquer

250 ml ketchup · 60 ml cider vinegar · 60 g molasses · 40 g brown sugar · 1 tbsp Worcestershire · 1 tsp smoked paprika · 1 tsp garlic powder · pinch of cayenne. Simmer 15 min, low. Brush on ribs in the final 15 minutes only — sugar burns (chapter 2 rule, forever true).

Alabama white

250 g mayonnaise · 60 ml cider vinegar · 1 tbsp prepared horseradish · 1 tsp black pepper (be brave) · 1 tsp lemon juice · ½ tsp salt · pinch of cayenne and sugar. Whisk cold. Dunk smoked chicken straight off the pit, the way Big Bob Gibson's has since 1925.

9.3Rub theory — building your own

A rub is four jobs in one jar. Build it by function and you'll never need a recipe again:

The four layers

1. Salt — the only ingredient that penetrates (chapter 6.5). 2. Pepper & savory — coarse black pepper, garlic, onion: the bark builders. 3. Color & depth — paprika, chili powders; paprika is mostly paint, and that's a legitimate job. 4. Sugar — binds bark and balances, but burns above ~150°C pit temp. Sugar belongs on low & slow pork, not on hot & fast anything.

Three house blends (parts by volume)

Texas Dalmatian: 1 salt : 1 coarse pepper. Brisket, beef ribs. Done.
Pork sweet: 2 brown sugar : 1 salt : 1 paprika : ½ pepper : ½ garlic : ¼ cumin : ¼ cayenne. Butts and ribs.
Poultry crisp: 1 salt : 1 paprika : ½ pepper : ½ garlic : ¼ brown sugar. Skin-friendly — light on sugar, heavy on color.

9.4The sides canon

Sides at a barbecue aren't garnish — they're load-bearing. The canon exists because each one solves the richness of smoked meat a different way: acid, starch, cold, or sweet.

  • Pinto beans (Texas) — simmered with chopped brisket trim, onion, chili powder. Never sweet. Sweet beans are a Kansas City thing; in Texas they're evidence.
  • Coleslaw — the acid delivery system. Vinegar slaw in the Carolinas goes on the pulled pork sandwich, not beside it. That's not optional there.
  • Potato salad — mustardy in Texas, sweet-pickled in the South. Served cold against hot meat: the temperature contrast is the point.
  • Mac & cheese — the great unifier and the one side children will police harder than any judge.
  • Escabeche / pickles — pickled jalapeños, carrots and red onion; the Mexican-Texan gift that cuts fat like a knife. Quick-pickle red onion in lime juice while the brisket rests and you look like a genius.
  • White bread — still from the bag, still untoasted, still law (chapter 5). It's not bread, it's a utensil.
  • Banana pudding — vanilla wafers, custard, banana, whipped cream. The traditional announcement that nobody is getting up from the table soon.
The certified plate

One meat sliced to order, one vinegar element, one creamy-cold element, one starch, pickles, white bread, and sauce on the side. Get those ratios right and you can feed forty people with less stress than a dinner party for six.
