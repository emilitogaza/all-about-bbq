Chapter 8 — Advanced Track

🌍 World of Smoke

Every culture that ever had meat and fire invented barbecue, and most of them did it before Texas. A certified pitmaster should know the family tree — and steal from it shamelessly.

8.1The Americas — the deep roots

🇦🇷 Argentina & Uruguay — asado

The gaucho tradition of the Pampas: whole cuts — ribs (asado de tira), flank (vacío), sweetbreads, chorizo — cooked slowly beside or above hardwood embers, never flame, on the iron parrilla or splayed on a vertical cross (asado al asador). The asador shovels coals from a separate burn-down fire, controlling heat by distance. Seasoning: coarse salt. Sauce: chimichurri, served cold and argued about hot. Asado isn't a meal, it's a Sunday-long social institution — the Argentine word for host, asador, is a title of honor.

🇧🇷 Brazil — churrasco

Born with the herders of Rio Grande do Sul, who roasted skewered beef around open fires. The churrascaria rodízio — waiters circling with swords of picanha, carving tableside until you flip your card to red — turned it into a global export. Rock salt, fat-side out, and the honest belief that a good cut needs nothing else. You met picanha in chapter 4; this is its home.

🇲🇽 Mexico — barbacoa & al pastor

Barbacoa (chapter 5's ancestor): lamb or beef head wrapped in maguey leaves over an underground pit of coals, steaming overnight into taco perfection. And the great immigrant remix: al pastor — Lebanese immigrants brought the shawarma spit to Puebla in the 1930s, Mexico swapped lamb for chile-marinated pork, added pineapple, and created arguably the greatest taco on earth. Barbecue history in one dish: technique migrates, flavor localizes.

🇯🇲 Jamaica — jerk

The oldest barbecue story in the hemisphere. The Taíno smoked meat on green-wood frames (their word became barbacoa, then "barbecue"); escaped African slaves — the Maroons — fleeing into the Blue Mountains in the 1600s–1700s, cooked wild pig in covered pits over pimento (allspice) wood so the smoke wouldn't betray their camps. Scotch bonnet, allspice, thyme, scallion: jerk seasoning is that survival history made delicious. Cook it over pimento wood or don't call it jerk — Jamaicans are firm on this.

8.2Africa & Europe

🇿🇦 South Africa — braai

Not "a barbecue" — the braai, a national institution across every community and language in the country. Wood fire burned to coals (charcoal is tolerated, gas is a scandal), boerewors (coiled farmer's sausage, by law ≥90% meat), lamb chops, sosaties (Cape Malay marinated skewers), and braaibroodjies — grilled cheese-tomato-onion sandwiches finished over coals. September 24, Heritage Day, is officially celebrated as National Braai Day, with Desmond Tutu as its late patron. The rule: the fire-tender is sacred; everyone else stands around holding a drink and offering unwanted advice. Some things are universal.

🇹🇷 🇬🇷 The Eastern Mediterranean — the vertical & horizontal spit

The kebab family is antiquity's barbecue: skewered meat over charcoal appears in Homer. Ottoman cooks turned the spit vertical in the 1800s — döner — which emigrated into Greek gyros, Middle Eastern shawarma, and (via Puebla) al pastor. Meanwhile şiş (shish) kebab, Georgian mtsvadi, and Balkan ćevapi carry the horizontal tradition. One technique, forty nations, and the ancestor of half the world's street food.

8.3Asia & the Pacific

🇰🇷 Korea — gogi-gui

Tabletop live-fire as social architecture: bulgogi ("fire meat," soy-pear-garlic marinated beef, documented back to the Goguryeo era as maekjeok) and galbi (butterflied short ribs — your plate primal again, sliced thin instead of smoked for 9 hours). Everyone grills together, wraps meat in lettuce with ssamjang and garlic, and the meal is the event. Korean BBQ proves the opposite lesson of Texas: same fire, opposite philosophy — thin and fast and communal instead of thick and slow and patient.

🇯🇵 Japan — yakitori & yakiniku

Yakitori is precision barbecue: a chicken broken down into 30+ named skewers — thigh, skin, cartilage, tail, heart — grilled over binchōtan, a white charcoal so pure it burns nearly smokeless at searing heat, glazed with tare sauces maintained for decades (the "mother tare" pot at old shops is never emptied, only topped up — a living culture like a sourdough starter). The lesson for a pitmaster: respect for every part of the animal, and the discipline of doing one thing ten thousand times.

🇨🇳 China — char siu

Cantonese "fork roast": pork shoulder or belly strips lacquered with honey, soy, five-spice and fermented bean curd, hung on forks in a drum oven until the edges char to mahogany candy. The red-glazed strips hanging in Chinatown windows are a barbecue tradition centuries older than any smokehouse — and char siu bao (the steamed bun) may be history's first great BBQ sandwich.

🇳🇿 Polynesia — hāngī, imu & kālua

The earth oven is the Pacific's pit: Māori hāngī, Hawaiian imu (whence kālua pig, the luau centerpiece), Fijian lovo. Stones heated in a fire, food wrapped in leaves, buried and steamed-smoked for hours. It's the same engineering as South Texas barbacoa and the Maroons' hidden pits — parallel invention, oceans apart, of the world's most patient cooking method.

The pattern a certified pitmaster should see

Every tradition on this page solves the same three problems — tough meat, fierce fire, and time — and lands on the same answers Texas did: indirect heat, smoke as seasoning, patience as technique. Barbecue wasn't invented anywhere. It was invented everywhere, and every style is a local accent of one universal language.

8.4Steal like a pitmaster

  • From asado: cook beside embers, not over flame, and serve chimichurri with your smoked beef — it cuts richness better than any BBQ sauce.
  • From jerk: allspice berries in your smoke wood chips, and Scotch-bonnet heat balanced with sweet — try it on chicken thighs.
  • From Korea: the lettuce-wrap service — brisket burnt ends in a lettuce leaf with ssamjang is a genuine revelation.
  • From yakitori: keep a "mother tare" — save and reduce your rib glaze each cook, feeding it forward. It deepens forever.
  • From braai: the braaibroodjie. A cheese-tomato-onion sandwich toasted over your dying coals while the meat rests. Thank South Africa later.
