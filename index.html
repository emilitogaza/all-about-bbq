Ch. 1Meat 101 — Know Your Animal

Every piece of meat is just muscle, fat, and connective tissue — and the ratio of those three tells you everything about how to cook it. Muscles that worked hard during the animal's life (shoulders, legs, chest) are packed with a connective tissue called collagen. Cooked fast, collagen is chewy shoe leather. Cooked long and gently, it melts into gelatin — the silky, sticky richness that makes pulled pork pull and brisket wobble.

Muscles that barely worked (the back, along the spine) have little collagen and are naturally tender. They don't need time — they need heat, fast, and a hard sear.

The one rule of the whole course

Hard-working muscle → low & slow. Lazy muscle → hot & fast. Almost every decision at the pit follows from this.

The two paths of fire

🐢

Low & Slow

107–135°C, indirect heat, hours of smoke. For collagen-heavy cuts: brisket, pork shoulder, ribs. You're not cooking to a "doneness" — you're cooking until the collagen surrenders, usually an internal temp of 90–96°C and a probe that slides in like butter.

⚡

Hot & Fast

230°C+, direct heat, minutes not hours. For tender cuts: ribeye, chicken pieces, pork chops, flank steak. Here you do chase doneness — sear hard for the crust (the Maillard reaction), pull at temp, and always rest before slicing.

Smoke, wood & a few words you'll hear

  • The stall — big cuts stop climbing around 65–74°C internal for hours while evaporating moisture cools the meat. It's normal. Wait it out, or wrap.
  • The Texas crutch — wrapping meat in foil or butcher paper mid-cook to power through the stall and hold in moisture.
  • Bark — the dark, crusty exterior built from rub, smoke, and rendered surface. It is not burnt. It is the best part.
  • Smoke ring — the pink ring under the bark, formed by nitric oxide in smoke reacting with myoglobin. Purely cosmetic — and pure bragging rights.
  • Wood pairing — mild fruit woods (apple, cherry) for chicken and pork; oak and hickory for pork and beef; mesquite is beef-only territory unless you like eating a campfire.
  • Rest your meat — always. 5–10 min for steaks, 1+ hour (wrapped, in a cooler) for brisket and pork butt. Juices redistribute; sliced too early they end up on the board.
Gear note

Everything in this course works on an offset smoker, a kettle with charcoal banked to one side, a pellet grill, or a gas grill with a smoke tube. The meat doesn't care about your equipment nearly as much as the internet does. A reliable instant-read thermometer is the only non-negotiable purchase.
