Chapter 2

🐔 BBQ Chicken

The gateway meat — cheap enough to practice on, fast enough to cook on a weeknight, and unforgiving enough to teach you temperature control.

2.1Two meats in one bird

A chicken is really two different proteins wearing the same feathers. Breast meat is lean, fast-twitch muscle — almost no fat, almost no collagen. It's done at 71–74°C and turns to chalk a few degrees past that. Thighs and legs are dark, hard-working muscle with more fat and connective tissue — they're not just safe at 79–85°C, they're better there, because the extra heat renders the fat and softens the tendons.

Why this matters at the pit

Whole birds are a compromise: by the time thighs are perfect, the breast is over. Solutions: spatchcock the bird (cut out the backbone, flatten it) so everything cooks evenly and faster, or position the bird with legs toward the hotter side of the grill.

2.2The method: hotter than you think

Here's the counterintuitive part — chicken is not a low-and-slow meat. There's no collagen to render, so hours at 107°C buys you nothing but rubbery skin. Smoke chicken at 150–175°C. You still get plenty of smoke flavor (chicken absorbs it fast), the skin crisps instead of turning to bicycle inner tube, and the whole cook is done in 1–1.5 hours.

  • Dry brine the night before: salt the bird all over, leave it uncovered in the fridge. Seasons deep, dries the skin for crisping.
  • Wood: apple or cherry. Chicken takes on smoke easily — mesquite or heavy hickory will make it taste like an ashtray.
  • Sauce late: if you're glazing with BBQ sauce, brush it on in the last 10–15 minutes only. Sugar burns; you want lacquer, not charcoal.
  • Beer can chicken: beloved, fun, and mostly a myth — the beer barely steams. Spatchcocking beats it on every measure. We say this with love.

One dish to master

Smoked spatchcock chicken: dry brine overnight → rub with paprika, garlic, black pepper, brown sugar → smoke at 165°C over cherry wood, skin up → glaze the last 10 minutes → rest 10 minutes. This single recipe teaches airflow, temperature, and patience — everything you need before pork.
