Chapter 11 — The Reckoning

🔍 Sacred Cows

Barbecue is a craft handed down uncle-to-uncle, which means it carries fifty years of folklore alongside the wisdom. Food scientists have put the folklore on the scale — literally. Here's what didn't survive.

11.1Myths, measured

Myth"Searing seals in the juices."

The science The granddaddy of them all, coined by German chemist Justus von Liebig in the 1850s and repeated ever since. Food scientist Harold McGee settled it decades ago by doing the obvious: weighing steaks. Seared steaks lose as much or more moisture than unseared ones — a crust is browned protein, not a waterproof shell (press one and watch it leak). Sear anyway, but for the right reason: the Maillard reaction is flavor, not plumbing.

Busted — sear for flavor, not moisture
Myth"Leave the fat cap thick — it melts down into the meat and bastes it."

The science Muscle is ~75% water, and fat is hydrophobic — rendered fat physically cannot soak into muscle fiber; it rolls off the surface and into the fire. Dye-tracing and slice tests (popularized by Meathead Goldwyn's AmazingRibs.com research team) show surface fat goes down, not in. The marbling inside the meat is what bastes it. A thick cap just blocks smoke, rub, and bark — which is why chapter 7 trims it to 6 mm.

Busted — marbling bastes, the cap blocks
Myth"Always soak your wood chips first."

The science Weigh chips before and after a 24-hour soak: they absorb only a few percent of their weight in water, nearly all of it in the outer millimetre. What looks like extra "smoke" from soaked chips is steam, and the water just delays the wood reaching clean combustion temperature — so you get more of the dirty, acrid start and less of the good burn. Physicist Greg Blonder's measurements put this one to bed. Dry wood, small hot fire, thin blue smoke (chapter 6).

Busted — you're smoking your meat with steam
Myth"Beer can chicken — the beer steams the bird juicy from the inside."

The science Probes tell the sad truth: shielded inside a chicken cavity, the beer typically never gets near boiling — often not past 60–70°C — so there is almost no steam, and steam wouldn't carry hop flavor into meat anyway. Worse, the can blocks airflow through the cavity, so the inner thighs cook slower and less evenly. Both Meathead and J. Kenji López-Alt have run this experiment to the same verdict. Spatchcock beats the can on speed, evenness, and skin — the beer does its best work in the cook.

Busted — drink the beer, flatten the bird
Myth"Let the meat come to room temperature before it hits the grill."

The science Kenji López-Alt measured it: after two full hours on the counter, the center of a thick steak rose about 2–3°C — thermally irrelevant, and a big roast changes even less. Meanwhile chapter 6 taught you the counter-argument: cold surfaces attract more smoke and build better rings. For smoked meat, straight from the fridge is actively better.

Busted — the counter rest does ~nothing
Myth"No smoke ring, no smoke flavor. Big ring? Great barbecue."

The science You know the chemistry from chapter 6.3: the ring is nitric oxide meeting myoglobin — a color reaction, not a flavor one. The kicker: you can fake a perfect ring with a pinch of pink curing salt and no smoke at all, and an electric smoker can produce deeply smoky meat with no ring (too little NO). This is precisely why KCBS instructs competition judges to ignore the ring entirely.

Busted — the ring is paint, not flavor
Myth"Meat stops taking smoke after the first couple of hours."

The science A confusion of two different processes. What stops early is ring formation (the surface passes ~60°C and myoglobin denatures). But smoke flavor — the phenols and carbonyls landing on the surface — deposits for the entire cook; that's measurable in the bark's chemistry. Ten hours of thin blue smoke tastes like ten hours, not two.

Busted — the ring quits early, flavor never does
Myth"Chicken's done when the juices run clear."

The science Juice color tracks myoglobin chemistry, not safety. Young commercial birds can bleed pink juice at perfectly safe temperatures (and bone-adjacent meat stays pink forever), while overcooked chicken can run clear long past sawdust. Smoked chicken adds the ring effect on top. The USDA's position is the same as chapter 1's: 71°C in the breast, measured, full stop. Color is a rumor; temperature is a fact.

Busted — thermometers over vibes
Myth"Pink pork will make you sick."

The science The fear dates from the trichinosis era — a parasite essentially eradicated from commercial pork decades ago (US cases are now a literal handful per year, almost all from wild game). The USDA updated its guidance back in 2011: whole-muscle pork is safe at 63°C with a 3-minute rest — which is juicy and pink. Grandma's gray pork chops were a public-health measure for a problem that no longer exists.

Busted — since 2011, officially
Myth"Flip the steak once. Only once. Touching it more is disrespect."

The science Side-by-side trials (Kenji, McGee, Blonder — rare unanimity) show flipping every 30–60 seconds cooks a steak faster and more evenly: neither surface has time to overheat or fully cool, so the gray overcooked band shrinks and the crust still develops fine. The single-flip rule is pure machismo. Flip as often as you like — the steak isn't offended.

Busted — flip early, flip often
Myth"Bone-in tastes better — the marrow flavors the meat while it cooks."

The science Flavor molecules don't migrate through solid bone into muscle, and blind taste tests find no detectable difference between bone-in and boneless steaks cooked to the same temperature. What the bone actually does is insulate: meat next to it cooks slower and stays juicier, which tasters then attribute to bone magic. Buy bone-in for the presentation and the caveman joy — both legitimate reasons — not the chemistry.

Busted — it's insulation, not seasoning
Myth"Marinate it overnight so the flavor gets deep inside."

The science Dye-penetration tests show most marinade flavor molecules travel 1–3 millimetres in 24 hours — they're simply too large to diffuse through muscle. The exceptions: salt (chapter 6.5) and, slowly, acid — and too long in acid turns the surface to mush. Marinades are excellent surface treatments, which is why they shine on thin, open-grained cuts like flank and skirt and do nearly nothing for the middle of a roast.

Mostly busted — it's a surface game

11.2Fun facts to deploy around the pit

  • Henry Ford invented the charcoal briquette business — Model T frames were built on wooden rails, and Ford turned the scrap into briquettes with his relative-by-marriage E.G. Kingsford. That's the same Kingsford on the blue bag today.
  • "Barbecue" is a Caribbean word. Taíno barbacoa → Spanish → English, with written English uses back to the 1600s. Texas adopted barbecue; it didn't coin it.
  • Brisket was once nearly free. Into the 1990s it was among the cheapest cuts in the case — the craft boom inverted the price. The cut didn't change; the demand did. (The same fate previously befell skirt steak after fajitas and flank after London broil.)
  • The "stall" wasn't scientifically explained until 2010 — pitmasters argued collagen vs. fat rendering for decades until Greg Blonder's evaporative-cooling experiments (chapter 6.4). Sometimes the uncles are wrong for fifty years.
  • Competition judges eat with their eyes first anyway: KCBS boxes are scored on appearance before anyone takes a bite — which is why competition ribs are glazed like furniture and backyard ribs taste better.
  • The Maillard reaction has a birthday: described by French chemist Louis-Camille Maillard in 1912. Humanity browned meat for a million years before anyone knew why it was delicious.
The certified attitude

Notice the pattern: every busted myth survived because nobody weighed anything. The pitmasters this course keeps citing — Blonder, McGee, Kenji, Meathead — earned their authority with scales, probes, and dye tests, not volume. Tradition tells you where to start; measurement tells you what's true. Hold both and you'll out-cook anyone who holds only one.
