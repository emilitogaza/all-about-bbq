Chapter 3

🐖 BBQ Pork

The heart of American barbecue. When the Carolinas, Memphis, and half the world's smoke traditions argue about barbecue, they're mostly arguing about pig.

3.1The shoulder — pulled pork

The pork shoulder is the perfect beginner's low-and-slow cut: huge, cheap, marbled throughout, and almost impossible to ruin. The upper half is sold as the Boston butt (yes, it's the shoulder — "butt" comes from the barrels, called butts, that colonial New England packed pork into). The lower half is the picnic shoulder.

Smoke a butt at 121°C for 8–12 hours to an internal of 95°C, wrapping through the stall around 74°C. Rest it at least an hour, then pull it apart with your hands — the bone should slide out clean. That's collagen turned to gelatin, exactly as promised in chapter 1.

Regional religion

Eastern North Carolina cooks the whole hog and dresses it in vinegar-pepper sauce. Lexington style smokes shoulders with a ketchup-tinged "dip". South Carolina runs mustard sauce. Pick a side, defend it loudly — that's tradition too.

3.2Ribs — know your racks

Baby backs

From up near the spine. Smaller, leaner, meatier between the bones, quicker to cook (~4–5 hrs). The crowd-pleaser rack.

Spare ribs

From the belly side. Bigger, fattier, more flavor, more collagen — the pitmaster's choice. ~5–6 hrs at 121°C.

St. Louis cut

Spare ribs squared off — cartilage tips trimmed away for a neat, even rack that cooks uniformly. The competition standard.

You'll hear about 3-2-1: three hours of smoke, two hours wrapped in foil, one hour unwrapped with sauce. It works, and it's a fine training-wheels method — but it's tuned for spares and tends to overcook baby backs to mush. Better habit: cook to feel. Ribs are done when the rack bends until the surface cracks and the meat has pulled back a quarter-inch from the bone ends.

  • Peel the membrane off the bone side before seasoning — it never renders and blocks smoke. A butter knife and a paper towel is all you need.
  • Memphis dry style: no sauce, ever — just a paprika-heavy rub reapplied at the end. Sauce on the side, if you must.
  • Wood: hickory is pork's soulmate. Apple + hickory blended is even better.

3.3Belly, loin & the rest of the pig

Low & slow Pork belly

Bacon's raw form — layers of fat and muscle. Cubed, smoked, tossed in sauce and butter, and smoked again, it becomes pork belly burnt ends: meat candy, and the fastest way to win a backyard.

Hot & fast Chops & tenderloin

Lean, lazy muscle — treat like steak. Thick-cut chops seared hard and pulled at 60–63°C are safely pink and juicy. The old "cook pork to death" rule died decades ago; let it rest in peace.

One dish to master

The Boston butt. Rub with salt, brown sugar, paprika, garlic, cumin → smoke at 121°C over hickory → wrap in butcher paper at 74°C → pull off at 95°C → rest one hour wrapped in a towel in a dry cooler → shred, splash with vinegar sauce, serve on cheap white buns with slaw. Feeds a crowd for pocket change.
